A digital humanities project on Korean women-oriented web novels, romance fantasy clichés, and hashtag networks as structures of desire.

One of the most common criticisms of romance fantasy web novels is their reliance on clichés. Hashtags such as #competentheroine, #girlcrush, #kindmalelead, #regretfulmalelead, #regression, and #contractmarriage may seem to point to the repetitive reproduction of standardized characters and plot structures.

These coded elements often make romance fantasy web novels appear less like “literature” and more like commercial products designed to provide readers with immediate catharsis.

Romance fantasy may be realistic precisely because of its clichés.

A cliché is not merely an old or repetitive narrative formula. It is also a condensed form of desire, repeatedly selected, consumed, and reproduced by readers. In romance fantasy web novels, hashtags function as codes that break down stories into smaller elements, such as the female protagonist, the male protagonist, relationships, conflicts, narrative devices, and forms of reward.

Readers use these codes to find the stories they want, platforms use them to classify works, and writers and markets recombine them to produce new stories.

Project Overview

From the perspective of digital humanities, these hashtags can be understood not simply as search terms, but as meaningful units for distant reading. The web novel market operates through continuous reader engagement, recurring payments, and immediate audience response. For this reason, hashtags do more than describe the content of individual works. They can also be read as indicators of what kinds of characters, relationships, and narrative rewards readers desire.

Lie That Tells the Truth 2.0 analyzes the relationships among hashtags used in contemporary Korean women-oriented web novels and examines the desires and gendered imaginations embedded within them. In women-oriented web novels, a single hashtag rarely produces meaning on its own. Rather, multiple hashtags are used together to construct a specific character type, relationship dynamic, or narrative structure.

Through hashtag network analysis, this project explores how the repeated codes of romance fantasy reveal the desires, anxieties, expectations, and idealized subjectivities of contemporary women readers.

Methodology

Dataset

The dataset was built from Korean romance fantasy webnovels and their associated platform hashtags. Each work was assigned a unique work ID, and each hashtag attached to that work was recorded as a separate work-tag relationship.

Data Preparation

The original dataset was reorganized into three CSV files: works.csv, work_tags.csv, and tag_metadata.csv. Hashtag symbols were removed for consistency, and non-informative values were excluded from the network.

Network Construction

Each node represents a hashtag. An edge is created when two hashtags appear in the same work. Edge weight represents the number of works in which two hashtags appear together.

Tool Design

The interactive explorer was built with D3.js. The network is calculated from the CSV files in the browser, allowing users to filter categories, hide dominant tags, inspect nodes, and compare selected hashtags.

Close Reading and Distant Reading

This approach does not replace close reading with distant reading. Instead, it places the two methods in a complementary relationship. While close reading allows us to examine the meanings of characters and narratives within individual texts, hashtag network analysis reveals recurring patterns and structures of desire across a larger body of works.

By connecting these two modes of reading, this project argues that romance fantasy web novels are not merely escapist fantasies. Rather, they are “lies that tell the truth,” fictional worlds that reveal how contemporary Korean women readers imagine, negotiate, and respond to the realities of gender, power, and social institutions.

Limitations and Data Ethics

This project does not claim that hashtags fully represent the content of a work. Platform tags are shaped by discoverability, marketing, reader expectations, and genre convention. They are useful cultural data, but they should not be treated as neutral or complete descriptions of the text itself.

The category labels used in this project also involve interpretation. Some hashtags can belong to multiple conceptual groups, and some may shift meaning depending on platform context or reader usage. For this reason, the network should be read as an exploratory map rather than a final classification of the genre.
